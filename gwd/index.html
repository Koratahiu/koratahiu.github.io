GWD
Korata Hiu*

Abstract

Standard decoupled weight decay (AdamW style) applies a uniform, spherical $L_2$ penalty to all parameters. However, modern optimizers like Adam, SignSGD, and SinkSGD do not operate in a spherical geometry; they aggressively scale, or precondition gradients. Applying a Euclidean penalty in a non-Euclidean optimization space distorts the objective and fights the optimizer's preconditioner.

Geometric Weight Decay (GWD) maps the weight decay penalty through the exact same geometry used by the optimizer's update step. This accelerates convergence, prevents the optimizer from unraveling useful structural representations, and widens the optimal hyperparameter range. We introduce GWD implementations for Adam (Fisher WD), SignSGD (Signed L1), and SinkSGD (Structural balance), alongside a mathematical normalization trick to guarantee drop-in compatibility with existing standard weight decay hyperparameters.

§ 0 The Problem with $L_2$ Regularization

Weight decay is conventionally implemented as an $L_2$ penalty: $\lambda \| \theta \|_2^2$, which yields a gradient of $2\lambda \theta$. In the context of standard SGD, this pulls all weights toward zero at a rate proportional to their magnitude. The parameter space is treated as perfectly isotropic (spherical).

But adaptive optimizers warp this space. Adam stretches coordinates inversely proportional to gradient variance. SignSGD maps the entire continuous space onto a discrete hypercube. Sinkhorn optimizers impose matrix-level structural constraints. When you apply a standard $L_2$ decay step inside these optimizers, you are applying a spherical prior to a warped space. The regularizer actively fights the preconditioner.

The Core Principle: If an optimizer scales the gradient $g$ by a preconditioning matrix $P^{-1}$, it must also scale the weight decay penalty by the same $P^{-1}$. The prior must live in the same geometry as the likelihood.

§ 1 Adam: Fisher Weight Decay

Adam pre-conditions updates using the square root of the uncentered variance, $v_t$. In a natural gradient context, $v_t$ acts as a diagonal approximation of the empirical Fisher Information Matrix. Standard decoupled weight decay (AdamW) bypasses this preconditioner:

$$ \theta_{t} = \theta_{t-1} - \eta \left( \frac{m_t}{\sqrt{v_t} + \epsilon} + \lambda \theta_{t-1} \right) \quad \text{(AdamW)} $$

Notice that the gradient $m_t$ is scaled by $1/\sqrt{v_t}$, but the penalty $\lambda \theta_{t-1}$ is not. To geometrically align the weight decay, we must map the decay step through the same empirical Fisher diagonal:

Geometric Alignment
$$ \theta_{t} = \theta_{t-1} - \eta \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{v_t} + \epsilon} \odot \left[ m_t + \lambda \theta_{t-1} \right] \right) $$

By pulling the $1/\sqrt{v_t}$ term out, the effective penalty applied to the weights becomes $\lambda / \sqrt{v_t}$. We call this Fisher Weight Decay.

  • Noisy/Uncertain parameters (High $v_t$): The decay is suppressed. The optimizer recognizes the signal is noisy and protects the weight from being aggressively decayed.
  • Confident/Consistent parameters (Low $v_t$): The decay is amplified. The optimizer is highly certain about this coordinate, so it can afford to regularize it strictly.
Fig. 1 — Fisher GWD vs Standard AdamW Decay
Standard WD applies a flat 1.0 multiplier regardless of variance. Fisher GWD applies an inverse-square-root curve, protecting noisy weights and penalizing certain ones.

§ 2 SignSGD: Signed $L_1$ Penalty

SignSGD discards gradient magnitudes entirely, outputting only $\pm 1$ updates. The underlying assumption is that the geometry of the optimization space is a discrete hypercube. Applying standard continuous $L_2$ weight decay ($\lambda \theta$) introduces arbitrary, continuous magnitude scaling back into the update, breaking the scale invariance of the optimizer.

If the gradient update maps to $\operatorname{sign}(g)$, the corresponding geometric weight decay must map to the same discrete space:

$$ \text{target}_{\text{GWD}} = \operatorname{sign}(\theta) $$

Instead of decaying weights by a percentage of their magnitude, GWD for SignSGD pulls weights towards zero by a constant, discrete step, exactly mirroring how the optimizer navigates the loss landscape. This equates to an $L_1$ penalty in the original loss formulation.

Fig. 2 — Signed L1 (GWD) vs Proportional L2 (Standard)
For SignSGD, GWD asserts a constant $\pm 1$ pressure (gold), matching the optimizer's $\pm 1$ gradients, whereas L2 decay (blue) asserts pressure based on magnitude.

§ 3 SinkSGD: Structural Balance

SinkSGD utilizes Sinkhorn iterations to uniformly distribute update magnitudes across the rows and columns of a weight matrix. It normalizes by row norms ($\|R_i\|$) and column norms ($\|C_j\|$). A standard $L_2$ weight decay ignores this structure, blindly penalizing weights regardless of their position in the matrix.

GWD for SinkSGD constructs a Structural Scaler based on the exact same row and column norms used during the gradient preconditioning:

$$ \text{scaler}_{i,j} = \sqrt{\|R_i\|_2 \cdot \|C_j\|_2} $$
Applied element-wise to the matrix.

This penalty is highly structural. If a weight belongs to a dominant, highly utilized row and a highly utilized column, the scaler is large, and the weight is penalized heavily. Conversely, weights in underutilized rows/columns are shielded from decay, naturally balancing the representational capacity of the network layer.

Fig. 3 — SinkSGD Structural Scaler
The penalty applied to a weight scales with the geometric mean of its row and column norms. Weights in highly utilized (dominant) paths face stronger regularization.

§ 4 The Transfer Trick (Normalization)

A major practical issue with introducing new weight decay formulas is that it breaks existing hyperparameters. If a user found $\lambda = 0.01$ optimal for AdamW, switching to Fisher GWD changes the global magnitude of the decay, requiring a complete retuning of $\lambda$.

To solve this, we introduce a mathematical trick: Norm Matching. We dynamically scale the GWD penalty so that its global strength (average magnitude) is exactly equal to the standard L2 penalty. This ensures zero-shot hyperparameter transferability.

For Directional Targets (SignSGD):

We scale the new geometric target to match the $L_2$ norm of the original parameter tensor:

$$ \widetilde{D}_{\text{geo}} = D_{\text{geo}} \cdot \frac{\|\theta\|_2}{\|D_{\text{geo}}\|_2} $$

For Scalers (Adam / SinkSGD):

We normalize the scaler matrix so its mean is exactly $1.0$. This guarantees that, on average, the exact same amount of weight decay is applied as in standard AdamW, just redistributed according to the geometry.

$$ \widetilde{S} = \frac{S}{\operatorname{mean}(S)} $$
Algorithm — Generic GWD Implementation
// 1. Adam (Fisher WD Scaler)
wd_scaler = 1.0 / sqrt(v_t)
wd_scaler = wd_scaler / wd_scaler.mean() ← The Transfer Trick
update = (m_t / sqrt(v_t)) + (λ * wd_scaler * θ)

// 2. SignSGD (Signed Target)
target = sign(θ)
target = target * (norm(θ) / norm(target)) ← The Transfer Trick
update = sign(g_t) + (λ * target)

// 3. SinkSGD (Structural Scaler)
scaler = sqrt(row_norms(θ) * col_norms(θ))
scaler = scaler / scaler.mean() ← The Transfer Trick
update = sinkhorn(g_t) + (λ * scaler * θ)
